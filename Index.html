2024 S650 // 2.3L TURBO I4 // R-1234YF

Key Diagnostic Clue: Recirculation Not Auto-Engaging

SYMPTOM: Recirc doesn't auto-activate when AC set to LO

On the 2024 Mustang, when you set the climate to LO and press AC, the system should automatically switch to recirculation mode. The fact that it stays on fresh (outside) air strongly suggests:

1. The AC compressor is NOT being commanded ON by the climate control module. The HVAC system knows the compressor isn't running, so it doesn't bother switching to recirc. This is the most likely scenario.

2. Why the compressor might not be commanded: Low refrigerant pressure. When R-1234yf charge drops below a minimum threshold, the low-pressure switch on the AC line tells the PCM to disable the compressor to prevent damage. The climate module then won't engage recirc because it knows cooling isn't happening.

3. The compressor clutch you felt spinning — are you sure it was the inner hub/plate engaging and not just the outer pulley free-spinning with the belt? The outer pulley always spins. The clutch engagement is a distinct "click" and the center plate starts turning with the pulley.

Updated Diagnosis Based on Recirc Clue

Recirc not auto-engaging = system knows AC compressor isn't ON

The 2024 Mustang's HVAC control module automatically enables recirculation when you request cooling. It's NOT doing that for you, which means the module is not commanding the compressor. The most common reason: low refrigerant tripping the low-pressure safety switch. Your car came from Flexcar with 30K miles — a slow leak over that time could easily drain the R-1234yf charge below the cutoff threshold.

Step-by-Step Diagnostic Checklist

Diagnostic Progress 0 / 8 steps complete
1
Verify Compressor Clutch Engagement (Re-check)
CRITICAL
LOCATION: Lower-center of engine bay, below the engine cover. Look down past the serpentine belt area (Images 8, 11, 14). The AC compressor is a cylindrical unit with a pulley on front.

How to test: Have someone sit inside and turn engine ON → set climate to LO → press AC button → set fan to max. Go to the engine bay and look at the compressor pulley. The outer ring always spins (belt-driven). You need to see if the center hub/plate clicks and starts spinning WITH the outer ring. If only the outer ring spins and the center stays still = clutch is NOT engaging = compressor is NOT running.

Better test: Listen for a "click" sound from the compressor area when someone toggles the AC button on/off from inside. You should hear a distinct electromagnetic click.

✓ PASS: Center plate engages with a click, spins with belt.
✗ FAIL: Only outer pulley spins, center is stationary → Low refrigerant or electrical fault preventing engagement.
2
Check Condenser Fan Operation
CRITICAL
LOCATION: Front of car, behind the lower grille/bumper. Not directly visible from hood-up position — you need to look from the FRONT through the grille opening or from below.

How to test: Engine on, AC set to LO and max fan. Walk to the front of the car and listen. You should hear a fan whirring behind the grille. You can also try to look through the grille mesh or feel for air being pulled through the grille from the engine side.

The condenser fan must run to cool the high-pressure refrigerant. Without it, the AC system will shut down on high-pressure safety.

✓ PASS: Fan audibly running, air being pulled through grille.
✗ FAIL: No fan sound → Check fuse in the under-hood fuse box (red cover, PASSENGER side — Images 4, 8). Could be a blown fuse, bad fan relay, or failed fan motor.
3
Feel the AC Lines (Temperature Test)
CRITICAL
LOCATION: Two metal/aluminum lines running from the compressor area upward toward the firewall and condenser. The thicker line (suction/low-pressure) is ~3/4" diameter. The thinner line (liquid/high-pressure) is ~3/8" diameter. Visible in the driver-side area near the coolant reservoir (Images 2, 7, 12, 13).

How to test: Engine on, AC on max for at least 3-5 minutes. Carefully touch both lines (they're usually aluminum with rubber sections at connections).

Thick line (LOW pressure): Should be COLD — cold enough to have water condensation/sweating on it. Like touching a cold soda can.

Thin line (HIGH pressure): Should be warm to hot.

✓ PASS: Thick line cold & sweating, thin line warm → System has charge and is working. Problem is elsewhere (blend door, etc.)
✗ FAIL: Both lines at ambient/same temp → System is empty or compressor isn't running. Most likely LOW REFRIGERANT.
4
Check AC Fuses and Relays
HIGH
LOCATION: Under-hood fuse box — PASSENGER SIDE (right), red cover visible in Images 4, 8, 14. Pop the red cover off. The fuse diagram is printed on the inside of the cover.

What to check: Look for fuses labeled "A/C CLUTCH", "A/C COMP", "HVAC", "BLOWER", and "COOLING FAN". Pull each one out and visually inspect — a blown fuse will have a broken wire inside. Also check relays in the same box — the AC clutch relay can fail. You can swap it with an identical relay from a non-critical circuit to test.

✓ PASS: All fuses intact, relays clicking when AC is toggled.
✗ FAIL: Blown fuse → Replace with same amperage. If it blows again, there's a short circuit — go to a shop.
5
Check Refrigerant Pressure (Gauge Test)
CRITICAL
LOCATION: Low-pressure service port is on the thicker (suction) AC line, DRIVER SIDE of engine bay. It has a small plastic cap (blue or black). Near the area between the compressor and the coolant reservoir (around where Images 12-13 were taken).

What you need: An R-1234yf gauge/recharge kit. Available at AutoZone, O'Reilly, or Amazon ($50-70). Make sure it's specifically R-1234yf — NOT R-134a (different connector, different refrigerant).

How to test: Engine off first. Connect the gauge to the low-pressure port (it only fits one way). Read the static pressure. Then start the engine, turn AC to max, and read running pressure.

Static pressure (engine off): Should read roughly equal to ambient temp in PSI (e.g., ~70°F ≈ ~70 PSI). If it reads 0 or near 0, the system is empty.

Running pressure (engine on, AC max): Low side should read 25-45 PSI on a working system.

Your charge spec (from Image 1 label): R-1234yf, 21.0 oz (0.595 kg). PAG oil WSS-M2C300-A2.

✓ PASS: Static ~60-80 PSI, running 25-45 PSI → Charge is OK, problem is electrical/mechanical.
✗ FAIL: 0 PSI or very low → System leaked out. Need professional recharge + leak detection (UV dye test).
6
OBD-II Scan for AC DTCs
HIGH
LOCATION: OBD-II port is under the dash, driver side, near the steering column. Plug in any OBD-II scanner.

What to do: If you have a Bluetooth OBD-II scanner (like an ELM327 or BlueDriver), plug it in and scan for DTCs (Diagnostic Trouble Codes). Look for codes like:

B-codes (Body/HVAC): B1A45, B1A46, B1A47 — relate to blend door actuators. B-codes starting with B10xx or B11xx relate to HVAC control.

P-codes (Powertrain): P0530 (AC pressure sensor), P0532/P0533 (AC pressure low/high), P0645 (AC clutch relay), P1464 (AC demand out of range).

AutoZone and O'Reilly will scan your codes for free in the parking lot.

✓ PASS: No AC-related DTCs → Problem is physical (low charge or mechanical).
✗ FAIL: DTC present → Tells you exactly what the PCM thinks is wrong. Narrows diagnosis significantly.
7
Check Cabin Air Filter
MEDIUM
LOCATION: INSIDE the car. Open the glove box → squeeze the sides to drop it down past the stops → you'll see the cabin filter housing behind it. Pull the filter out.

What to look for: If it's clogged with leaves, dirt, or debris, airflow will be severely restricted. This won't fix the "not cold" issue but will improve air volume. At 30K miles from Flexcar, it may have never been changed.

A clean filter won't make the air cold — this is supplementary to the main issue. But worth checking since you're troubleshooting everything.

✓ PASS: Filter is clean/light gray → Airflow is fine.
✗ FAIL: Filter is dark/clogged → Replace ($15-20, available at any auto parts store). Part: Motorcraft FP-79.
8
Check for Visible Leaks (Oil Residue)
HIGH
LOCATION: Inspect all AC line connections, especially at the compressor fittings, condenser connections (front of car), and where lines pass through the firewall. Also check around the receiver/dryer (small canister near the condenser).

What to look for: Refrigerant oil (PAG oil) is a greenish-yellow oily residue. Where refrigerant leaks, it evaporates but leaves behind the oil. Look for any oily/greasy spots on the AC line fittings, especially at O-ring connections.

Common leak points on the 2024 Mustang: condenser (rock damage from road debris), compressor shaft seal, and O-ring fittings at line connections.

✓ PASS: No oily residue at connections → Leak may be very slow or at the condenser (hard to see).
✗ FAIL: Oily green residue at a fitting → You found your leak. A shop can confirm and replace the O-ring or component.

Most Likely Diagnosis Summary

VERDICT: LOW REFRIGERANT (R-1234yf) — 85% probability

Given that the recirc doesn't auto-engage (HVAC module isn't commanding AC), the compressor clutch may not actually be engaging (re-verify), and the car has 30K miles from a fleet service — the most likely issue is that the system has lost its R-1234yf charge through a slow leak. The low-pressure safety switch is preventing the compressor from running to protect it from damage.

What to tell the shop: "AC blows ambient air, recirc doesn't auto-engage when set to LO, suspect low refrigerant. Need leak test and recharge with R-1234yf (21.0 oz spec). Car has 30K miles, first summer use."

Expected cost: Recharge only (if no major leak): $150-250. Recharge + leak detection + minor repair (O-ring): $250-500. Condenser replacement (if punctured): $500-900.
